Bridging India’s immunization gap with wearables and data

Every year, hundreds of thousands of Indian children die from vaccine preventable diseases. Khushi Baby (ख़ुशी बेबी, happy baby in hindi), a startup based in Udaipur, has decided to leverage new technologies to try to bridge the immunization gap. To grasp the importance of their work, one has to understand the gravity of the situation they are striving to solve. We hope you find the information below insightful, and take the time to check out Khushi Baby’s work.

Infant mortality is a real problem in India

According to the WHO, 5.6 million children under the age of 5 years died in 2016. This translates into 15 000 under-five deaths per day. More than half of these early child deaths are due to conditions that could be prevented or treated with access to simple, affordable interventions. Leading causes of death in children under-5 years are preterm birth complications, pneumonia, birth asphyxia, diarrhoea and malaria. India is no exception. Despite the Indian government’s efforts to reduce the infant mortality rate, many children still die every year from poor sanitation and a lack of access to basic medical needs. India’s infant mortality is 6 times higher than that of China, and 10 times higher than in France. Rural and isolated areas are the most affected.

1,5 million children die every year

India has experienced impressive improvements in its economic status and population health during the past two decades. However, it still lags behind other countries of similar per capita GDP in terms of child survival. Although India is a leading producer and exporter of vaccines, the country is home to one-third of the world’s unimmunized children. 1,5 million children die every year from vaccine-preventable diseases, out of which 500,000 are Indian.

This is due to the lack of health and sanitary infrastructure…

According to The Hindu (Indian newspaper), around 80% of doctors serve 28% of India’s population. The country’s health infrastructure is inadequate to serve its large population, especially in rural and remote areas. Many have to walk many kilometers on foot in order to reach the nearest health facility. As a result, many Indian women living in rural and remote areas are isolated from proper medical care, making them more likely to have unmonitored pregnancies.

... and the low awareness about the importance of vaccination

The vaccination problem in India also lays in the demand side – meaning the degree to which individuals do their part to be vaccinated.
Poor education levels, which are consistently correlated with the likelihood that individuals will not complete vaccination schedules, pose a major barrier to expanding vaccination rates in rural areas.

Khushi Baby is actively tackling this issue

Khushi Baby is an Indian startup addressing the current situation with a necklace that digitizes pregnant mothers’ medical history. Linked to an app accessible on a tablet, it enables nurses to have immediate access to the patient’s health records. The Khushi Baby system is also a powerful social symbol and a potential driver of behavioral change.

The Startup

KhushiBaby's mission is to motivate and monitor the health care of mothers and children to the last mile. That's why they built an inexpensive digital necklace that makes medical history wearable.

The solution

KhushiBaby's solution is a necklace that contains a NFC chip in which the patient's basic medical information is stored. This necklace costs only 1$ so that families can afford it. The nurses just have to scan the chip with their tablet in order to access and modify the information. This is an innovative user-center product, that brings the patient back at the heart of the solution, by letting her carry her information around her neck.

Their impact

Today, Khushi Baby is mostly present in villages in Rajasthan and Gujarat. The startup has already enrolled 12,000 beneficiaries (and given out as many necklaces), works with 85 ANMs (local nurses) and has rolled out its program across 365 villages.

What’s next ? Objectives for 2018

Khushi Baby’s objectives for 2018 are ambitious but feasible, considering the speed at which the startup is expanding across the country. In the next coming months, Khushi Baby hopes to roll around across 1000 villages, to reach 30,000 beneficiaries and to have enrolled 250 ANMs (local nurses) in their system.
