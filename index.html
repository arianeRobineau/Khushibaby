Infant mortality is a real problem in India

According to the WHO, 5.6 million children under the age of 5 years died in 2016. This translates into 15 000 under-five deaths per day. More than half of these early child deaths are due to conditions that could be prevented or treated with access to simple, affordable interventions. Leading causes of death in children under-5 years are preterm birth complications, pneumonia, birth asphyxia, diarrhoea and malaria. India is no exception. Despite the Indian government’s efforts to reduce the infant mortality rate, many children still die every year from poor sanitation and a lack of access to basic medical needs. India’s infant mortality is 6 times higher than that of China, and 10 times higher than in France. Rural and isolated areas are the most affected.

This is the reality

Compared to other countries, India’s percentage of fully immunized children is very low. Though it has improved over the past decade, 38% of Indian children aged 12 to 23 months are still not fully immunized (for common and life threatening diseases such as polio or measles). This situation has two main causes: the lack of health and sanitary infrastructures, and low awareness about the importance of pregnancy monitoring and child vaccination.
According to the WHO, a child's risk of dying is highest in the first 28 days of life (the neonatal period). Improving the quality of antenatal care, care at the time of childbirth, and postnatal care for mothers and their newborns are all essential to prevent these deaths. This is exactly what Khushi Baby, a social venture based in Udaipur, is striving to do in India.

Lack of access

Indian mothers have very few access to medical care in the country. As a matter of fact, there are only 0,2 doctors for 1000 persons, and even if this number is increasing it's still not enough for the 376 inhabitants per km²
